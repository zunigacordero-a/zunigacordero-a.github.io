I am a Marie Skłodowska-Curie COFUND–C2W Postdoctoral Researcher at the University of Namur. I earned my PhD in Economics from the Paris School of Economics in 2024, following the APE master’s programme, and spent 2021–2022 as an exchange researcher at the London School of Economics and the Centre for Economic Performance.

Before moving to France, I worked in financial markets and later in the Research Department of the Central Bank of Costa Rica. I hold bachelor’s degrees in Economics and Sociology from the University of Costa Rica, and an IB Diploma from Li Po Chun UWC of Hong Kong.

My research studies how inequality, politics, trade, migration, and climate change shape one another. I am currently examining how globalisation and inequality affect electoral outcomes, with new projects on the political effects of extreme weather events and climate policies.

I have consulted for the World Bank and the United Nations ECLAC, I serve as Coordinator for Central America and the Caribbean at the World Inequality Lab, and I am affiliated with the Programa Estado de la Nación in Costa Rica.

Research interests: economic inequality, political economy, trade, migration, applied econometrics.

Distributional National Accounts Costa Rica: Inequality from the National to the Local Level (2000–2022)
Solo-authored working paper, ready to submit. (8 citations, Google Scholar)

It revises standard inequality estimates, showing higher levels and sharper changes than in official statistics, especially around the COVID-19 crisis. Income Shocks, Economic Inequality, and Political Realignment: Voter Turnout and the Shift to a Multi-Party System in Costa Rica
With Ronald Alfaro-Redondo. Job-market paper. (1 citation, Google Scholar)
Abstract. This study links individual electoral registers with employer–employee social security data to analyse how income levels, mobility, and local inequality shape turnout and party support in Costa Rica. Electoral participation is positively related to income but the pattern is mediated by inequality: high-inequality contexts mobilise voters at the extremes, while more equal areas see higher participation among the middle. Downward income shocks boost short-run turnout, a result confirmed using quasi-experimental variation from mass layoffs and international price changes. The paper also shows that these dynamics contributed to the erosion of a traditional dominant party and to the emergence of a more fragmented party system. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Climate Change and Electoral Behaviour: Evidence from Extreme Weather Events in Costa Rica
With Lorenzo Trimarchi & Juan Robalino.
Abstract. Using geo-referenced data on disasters and high-resolution weather records matched to electoral outcomes and administrative employer–employee data, this paper studies how exposure to extreme weather affects voting behaviour in Costa Rica. A difference-in-differences design with staggered treatment tracks regions before and after shocks. Preliminary results indicate that direct experience with climate shocks raises political engagement, but the direction of partisan shifts depends on local socio-economic and ideological contexts. Progressive areas tend to increase support for green or pro-environmental platforms, whereas conservative regions show higher backing for evangelical populist alternatives, partly through the mobilisation of existing religious networks. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Databases

Many of my projects involve constructing and harmonising large administrative and survey datasets. Below is a selection of databases I have developed or contributed to.

Distributional National Accounts for Costa Rica (2000–2022)

Integrated microdata combining social security records, tax files, household surveys, census data, and national accounts to produce consistent national and subnational inequality measures.

Electoral & Socioeconomic Microdata, Costa Rica

Individual-level voter turnout and registration linked to employer–employee records, local inequality indicators, and polling-station information for presidential and municipal elections.

Climate & Extreme Weather Exposure Dataset, Costa Rica

Geo-localised data on extreme weather events and disasters matched to population, economic activity, and electoral outcomes to study the political consequences of climate shocks.

Inequality & Political Cleavages in Latin America (1952–2019)

Historical database on social inequalities, parties, and voting patterns in six Latin American countries, developed as part of the World Inequality Lab and Harvard University Press volume.
